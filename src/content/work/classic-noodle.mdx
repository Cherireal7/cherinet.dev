## The starting line

A neighborhood restaurant in Bishoftu, known by anyone within a few blocks, invisible to anyone outside them. The menu existed only on a printed board. There was a Facebook page nobody updated. Online orders came in by WhatsApp screenshot to a manager who also had to expedite the kitchen.

The brief was open: *do something with digital*. I treated it as a small product problem with a marketing surface.

## The work

- **Menu as a structured artifact**, not a photo. Categorized, priced, photographed consistently, and reusable across platforms.
- **A single ordering channel** — one WhatsApp Business number with a templated reply flow, so the manager stopped retyping the menu twenty times a day.
- **A weekly content rhythm** for social: one dish, one behind-the-scenes, one customer story. Scheduled in advance, batched in a single shoot.
- **An analytics frame** — order count by day-of-week, lead time per channel, repeat rate. Nothing fancy; the point was to have any number at all.

## What changed

Within the first quarter, online-channel orders grew by roughly **40%** off a small base. More importantly, the manager stopped being the bottleneck — the WhatsApp reply flow handled the first three exchanges, which freed her to expedite. The content cadence built a recognizable visual presence that the regulars started sharing.

## Why this is on a frontend portfolio

Because the lesson is the same as on a SaaS product: *the channel that works is the one with the lowest friction at the worst moment* — Friday dinner rush, in a kitchen, on a 4-year-old Android. Designing for that constraint sharpened how I think about onboarding flows on FIDA and post-purchase flows on BizBridge. The principles travel.

## What I actually did

Yeneta Master is a school management system used by Ethiopian institutions for student records, attendance, grades, fees, and communication. My job was not to build it — it was to **make it real** for two campuses that had been running on paper and Excel for fifteen years.

That meant: data migration from handwritten records, role-by-role training, payroll and fee module configuration, troubleshooting at six in the morning when a kindergarten teacher could not mark attendance, and weekly conversations with administrators who, reasonably, did not trust a screen.

## The operational lessons

The lessons I took into engineering:

- **Production is not deployment.** A feature is in production when the person who needs it can use it without help. Most software is not in production. Most of it is just deployed.
- **Empathy beats elegance.** The cleanest UI in the world fails if it does not match the mental model of the user. A "Mark Absent" button needs to be the size of a thumb on a phone held vertically in a classroom.
- **Edge cases are the product.** The default flow is what the demo shows. The hundred branches off that flow are what the user actually experiences over a school year — make-up exams, sibling discounts, scholarship overrides, mid-term transfers. If you do not design for those, you do not have a product, you have a slide.
- **Trust is earned weekly.** It took twelve months for administrators to retire the paper ledger. That is fine. They were right to be cautious.

## How this shows up in my code today

When I lead a UX decision on FIDA or BizBridge, I am not arguing from a Dribbble shot. I am arguing from a thousand small moments of watching real users hit the wrong button. That instinct came from this work.
